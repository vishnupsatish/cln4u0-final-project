Introduction

For my final project for grade 12 law, I decided to do an in-depth analysis on the Quebec City Mosque shooting case.

Earlier in the course, as part of the criminal law unit, I wrote a report on the ruling of Bissonnette's sentencing by the trial judge and the appeal judge. The report is shown below.

But numerous unanswered questions remained. Should a distinction be made between crimes in which someone commits one murder and someone commits numerous murders? Should the basis of unconstitutionality be the discretion provided to give a grossly proportionate sentence, or the sentence itself?

I needed a plan. First, I was going to read the original sentencing, the appeal, and the Supreme Court case (again). This time, I would take notes so I wouldn't forget important points from the documents. I would then create a list of questions I had about the case, s. 745.51, and how they relate to Canadian law. Finally, I would find an expert in the subject to interview who would answer the questions.

Finding experts

After reading the case and coming with relevant questions to ask, I had to find experts to interview. I mainly targeted law professors and Crown lawyers, as their emails were the easiest to find. In total, I emailed around 30 experts.

I received more responses than I expected. Responses were a mixed bag. Some responded stating that criminal law was not their expertise (although they had it in their list of interests on their university profile??), some responded saying the timeline was too tight, and others agreed to an interview.

Unfortunately (this is a good problem to have), there were too many affirmative responses! One Crown lawyer and two professors agreed to an interview. By the time the Crown lawyer agreed, I had already spoken to two professors, so I thanked the lawyer and informed her that I wouldn't need her help anymore.
